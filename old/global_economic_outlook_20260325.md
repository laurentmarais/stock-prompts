# Global Economic Outlook: Next Month

Date: March 25, 2026

## Bottom Line

The market is still trading the Middle East war first, but the balance has shifted again over the last 24 hours.

Stocks are trying to price a possible diplomatic off-ramp after reports that the U.S. sent Iran a 15-point proposal and that Pakistan or Turkey could host talks. Oil has dropped back below $100, bond yields have eased again, and equities are staging a relief rally.

But the physical energy system still does not look normal. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed for most commercial traffic, shipping protection looks far harder than the failed Red Sea playbook, and Asia is already moving into fuel-conservation and subsidy mode. So the market is currently trading negotiation hope on top of a still-unresolved supply shock.

My base case for the next month is:

- global equities stay volatile, but the short-term bias is less negative than it was a few days ago,
- oil remains above pre-war norms even if ceasefire hopes keep prices under the panic highs,
- yields remain sensitive to oil, but the immediate inflation panic has eased,
- the Fed stays on hold, while Europe and parts of Asia remain more vulnerable to a renewed energy shock,
- energy, defense, and selective value remain relatively supported, while consumer-sensitive and energy-import-heavy markets stay more fragile.

## What Is Happening Right Now

### 1. Markets are trading diplomacy hope, not a solved war

The biggest change since yesterday is that diplomacy looks marginally more credible at the same time that the fighting is still ongoing.

What changed:

- Reuters reported that Pakistan delivered a U.S. proposal to Iran.
- Pakistan or Turkey could host discussions as soon as this week.
- Turkey says it is actively passing messages between Washington and Tehran.
- Iran still publicly denies that negotiations are under way.

Why this matters for stocks:

- markets are willing to rally on even a partial chance of a ceasefire,
- but the rally is still based on headline risk rather than confirmed normalization,
- this means fast reversals remain likely if diplomacy stalls or attacks intensify.

### 2. The relief rally is real, but it is resting on falling oil rather than restored supply

The strongest immediate market reaction is in oil and rates.

Current market picture:

- Brent crude has fallen to roughly $98 to $99.
- WTI is back near $87 to $88.
- The U.S. 10-year Treasury yield has eased to about 4.33%.
- The 10-year German bund yield is back near 2.97%.
- European stocks are bouncing, with the STOXX 600 up roughly 1.4% on the day in the latest Reuters market report.

Why this matters for stocks:

- lower oil reduces the near-term inflation scare,
- lower yields help valuation support, especially in large-cap growth,
- but the move is fragile because the improvement is being driven by ceasefire hopes, not by a clean reopening of Hormuz.

### 3. The energy shock remains structurally unresolved

This is still the core macro problem.

What matters most:

- Hormuz remains effectively closed for most ships,
- roughly one-fifth of global oil and LNG supply normally moves through it,
- Reuters reporting now suggests securing the strait could take months, not days,
- the military challenge is more complex than the already-unsuccessful Red Sea escort effort,
- additional disruption is appearing outside the Gulf too, including halted Baltic oil loadings after Ukrainian drone strikes on Russian export infrastructure.

Why this matters for stocks:

- even with oil below $100 today, the market still has a large embedded supply risk,
- if talks fail, oil can snap higher again very quickly,
- if talks progress but shipping stays impaired, oil may stay elevated even without a full panic regime.

### 4. Asia is now the clearest near-term macro pressure point

Asia is on the front line of the fuel shock because it buys more than 80% of the crude that normally transits Hormuz.

What is happening:

- Japan is using subsidies to cap fuel-price pain and is asking the IEA for further stockpile releases,
- South Korea is openly discussing work-from-home policies,
- the Philippines has declared an energy emergency,
- Pakistan and Sri Lanka are already using austerity-style fuel-saving measures,
- Australia and New Zealand are also introducing relief and anti-price-gouging responses.

Why this matters for stocks:

- Asia remains the region most directly exposed to any prolonged disruption,
- consumer, airline, transport, and energy-import-heavy sectors remain vulnerable,
- growth-sensitive Asian markets can still lag even during global relief rallies.

### 5. Central banks are still stuck in an inflation-versus-growth dilemma

The war is no longer just a geopolitical problem. It is a policy transmission problem.

Fed:

- still on hold after the March 18 meeting,
- not positioned to rescue markets unless oil and inflation cool further,
- likely to remain reactive rather than proactive over the next month.

ECB:

- still expected by most economists to stay on hold this year,
- but the hawkish tail risk has risen sharply,
- more than a third of economists in the latest Reuters poll now expect at least one hike this year,
- inflation is now expected to remain above target until Q2 2027.

BOJ and Asia:

- imported inflation remains the key problem,
- authorities are using subsidies and emergency measures, but that does not eliminate the macro drag,
- energy shock management is becoming more administrative and fiscal rather than purely monetary.

## What Will Likely Change Over The Next Month

## Base Case

The next month will likely be driven by three variables:

- whether talks through Pakistan or Turkey become real negotiations,
- whether Hormuz traffic improves in practice rather than just rhetorically,
- whether oil can stay below $100 to $105.

My base case is still volatility, but it is no longer the same as the March 21 to March 24 panic phase. It is now a market trying to price a negotiated off-ramp while still carrying a large unresolved supply shock.

### 1. Equities can extend the relief rally, but only to a point

The short-term path of least resistance is higher if ceasefire hope keeps building.

That likely means:

- U.S. and European equities can rebound further near term,
- large-cap growth and cyclicals can participate as yields ease,
- but investors are unlikely to pay aggressively higher multiples until shipping and energy flows improve for real.

### 2. Oil remains the dominant market signal

This has not changed.

- Brent below $100 supports the relief trade.
- Brent in the $100 to $110 zone keeps the market cautious but stable enough for selective upside.
- Brent back above $110 reintroduces inflation and rate fear.
- Brent back into the $115 to $120 area would likely restart a stagflation-led selloff.

### 3. Europe remains more fragile than the U.S.

Europe benefits from lower oil and lower yields, but it remains structurally more exposed than the U.S.

- imported-energy sensitivity is still high,
- growth data is already softening,
- the ECB has less room to sound relaxed if second-round inflation fears grow,
- Italy and other fossil-fuel-import-heavy markets remain more vulnerable during renewed stress.

### 4. Asia remains the weakest major regional mix

This is the clearest regional conclusion for the next month.

- Japan and broader Asia remain highly exposed to fuel shortages and imported inflation,
- emergency conservation and subsidy measures are not bullish signals for equities,
- even if talks progress, Asia is likely to recover less cleanly than the U.S. because the direct fuel shock is larger.

## What Is Happening In The World Right Now

### 1. Middle East conflict is now a market problem

The war involving Iran has escalated into a direct threat to energy supply. Markets are focused on the Strait of Hormuz because roughly one-fifth of global crude oil and LNG flows normally pass through it. Recent reporting indicates:

- Brent crude is trading around $110 to $112 per barrel, after briefly pushing higher.
- U.S. crude is near $98 per barrel.
- Energy infrastructure damage in the Gulf has raised fears that supply disruption could last longer than markets first expected.
- The conflict is still escalating politically and militarily, which keeps headline risk high day to day.

Why this matters for stocks:

- Higher oil acts like a tax on consumers and businesses.
- It raises transport, manufacturing, and utility costs.
- It pushes inflation expectations higher.
- It reduces the chance of rate cuts that equity markets had been hoping for.

### 2. Central banks have shifted from easing bias to inflation defense

The Federal Reserve, ECB, Bank of England, Bank of Canada, and Bank of Japan all sound much more cautious because of the energy shock.

What changed:

- The Fed held rates steady, but markets have sharply reduced expectations for cuts.
- Some rate markets are now pricing a real possibility of further tightening if inflation broadens.
- The ECB has raised inflation concerns and is discussing the possibility of hikes if second-round inflation effects appear.
- The BOJ held at 0.75%, but still has a tightening bias because oil and yen weakness can feed inflation.
- China is keeping lending rates steady and appears likely to delay fresh easing unless growth weakens more clearly.

Why this matters for stocks:

- Stocks perform best when inflation is falling and rates are stable or declining.
- That setup has weakened.
- Higher yields reduce the valuation support for technology and long-duration growth shares.
- Credit-sensitive and highly leveraged sectors become more vulnerable.

### 3. Bond markets are signaling tighter financial conditions

Government bond yields have moved higher across the U.S. and Europe.

Current picture:

- U.S. 10-year Treasury yield is around 4.39%.
- German 10-year Bund yield has pushed above 3.0%.
- UK 10-year gilt yield is near 5.0%, a level that signals market stress around energy sensitivity and growth.

Why this matters for stocks:

- Higher yields mean a higher discount rate on future earnings.
- That tends to hit expensive equity sectors first.
- Higher borrowing costs also weigh on housing, consumers, private credit, and corporate refinancing.

### 4. Global growth is still holding up, but the shock is changing the near-term mix

The IMF still describes the world economy as broadly steady in 2026, with global growth around 3.3%, but geopolitical tension is now the main downside risk.

That means the next month is less about a sudden global recession and more about this transition:

- From soft-landing optimism
- To slower-growth, higher-inflation uncertainty

This is a worse backdrop for broad equity multiples even before earnings estimates fall.

## What Will Likely Change In The Next Month

## Base Case

Over the next month, markets will likely trade in a pattern driven by three variables:

- Oil price direction
- Bond yield direction
- Central bank repricing

If the conflict continues without a credible de-escalation, the most likely changes are:

### 1. Equity markets remain volatile, with rallies failing more easily

Recent market action suggests investors are not panicking, but they are de-risking. The S&P 500 has fallen for several straight weeks and broken important technical levels, including its 200-day moving average.

Over the next month, that usually means:

- Short-covering rallies can happen fast.
- But sustained upside is harder unless oil drops and yields retrace.
- Index-level performance is likely to be choppy rather than smoothly trending.

### 2. Leadership shifts away from broad growth and toward inflation hedges

Likely relative winners:

- Energy producers
- Defense names
- Some commodity exporters
- Select value stocks with pricing power

Likely laggards:

- High-multiple technology
- Consumer discretionary
- Airlines and transport
- Chemical, industrial, and manufacturing businesses with high energy input costs
- European import-heavy sectors

### 3. Europe remains especially exposed

Europe is one of the clearest risk areas for the next month because it is more exposed to imported energy costs and to any renewed gas stress.

Likely implications:

- European equities may underperform U.S. stocks on a relative basis.
- European bonds may stay under pressure if inflation expectations continue rising.
- Industrials, chemicals, autos, and consumer sectors face margin pressure.

### 4. Japan faces a difficult inflation-versus-growth tradeoff

Japan is vulnerable because higher oil prices worsen the terms of trade, while yen weakness increases imported inflation.

Over the next month:

- Japanese equities could stay volatile.
- Exporters may not benefit as much from yen weakness if global demand expectations fall.
- BOJ tightening expectations may remain alive, especially if inflation signals stay firm.

### 5. China may be steadier than other importers, but not immune

China appears less likely to respond immediately with major stimulus because recent activity data were firmer and policymakers are keeping rates steady.

Over the next month:

- Chinese equities may be relatively more stable than Europe if domestic policy support remains credible.
- But export-related and cyclical sectors still face downside if global demand and supply chains weaken.
- Any further commodity shock would still be a drag on sentiment and margins.

## What Traders Should Watch Most Closely

If you want to understand how the next month is likely to trade, focus on these indicators in this order:

### 1. Oil

This is the clearest real-time market barometer.

- If Brent moves back toward $100 or below, equity pressure should ease.
- If Brent pushes sustainably above $115 to $120 again, markets will likely price more inflation and less growth.

### 2. U.S. 10-year yield

This matters because it affects valuation across global equities.

- Above 4.3% to 4.5% keeps pressure on broad equity multiples.
- A retreat lower would help growth stocks recover.

### 3. Any signal of reopening or further disruption in Hormuz

This is the fastest way the market narrative changes.

- De-escalation would likely trigger a relief rally.
- Further attacks on shipping or energy facilities would hit risk assets immediately.

### 4. Central bank language

If officials keep emphasizing temporary inflation, markets can stabilize.
If they start openly discussing hikes, the selloff can broaden.

### 5. High-frequency growth data

Watch:

- PMI surveys
- consumer sentiment
- inflation data
- shipping and energy supply updates

Weak activity plus high energy prices would strengthen the stagflation narrative.

## Practical Conclusion

For the next month, the world economy is not primarily about normal growth forecasting. It is about whether an energy shock becomes persistent enough to keep inflation elevated and delay policy relief.

That means stock trading will likely be driven by macro sensitivity more than by company-specific fundamentals.

The most likely near-term outcome is:

- more volatility,
- weaker performance for broad equity indices,
- relative strength in energy and defense,
- and continued pressure on growth stocks unless oil and yields fall.

If the Middle East conflict cools, markets can recover quickly. If it does not, the next month is likely to look more like a stagflation scare than a clean risk-on environment.

## Summary In One Sentence

Right now the world’s main stock-market risk is that the Iran-driven energy shock pushes oil and bond yields high enough to keep inflation sticky, forcing central banks to stay hawkish and leaving equities volatile and biased lower over the next month.
